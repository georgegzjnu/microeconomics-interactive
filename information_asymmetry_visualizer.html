💡 Key Insight

When buyers cannot distinguish good cars from lemons, they are unwilling to pay the fair price for good cars. This causes good cars to exit the market, leaving only lemons. This is called the "Market for Lemons" problem.

Adverse Selection Effect

As quality uncertainty increases, buyers discount their willingness to pay, which disproportionately affects high-quality sellers. The market equilibrium shifts to an inefficient point where only lower-quality goods trade.

📋 Potential Solutions

  • Signaling: Sellers with good products invest in warranties, certifications, or reputation
  • Screening: Buyers design selection mechanisms (tests, waiting periods) to differentiate quality
  • Third-party certification: Trusted intermediaries verify product quality

Behavioral Impact

Higher insurance coverage reduces individual incentive to prevent losses (less cautious driving, less preventive health behavior). This increases the expected loss, which in turn increases insurance premiums, potentially causing a cycle of adverse selection and moral hazard.
